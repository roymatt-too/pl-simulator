## 5. Medium Excerpt Article (~800 words)

### What Most Foreigners Get Wrong About Sushi -- A Japanese Local's Perspective

I am Japanese. I have eaten sushi my entire life. And every time I see a foreign visitor drown a piece of carefully crafted nigiri in soy sauce, or mix a mountain of wasabi into their shoyu dish, or order nothing but salmon for an entire meal, a small part of me dies.

I do not say this to be pretentious. Sushi is a craft that Japanese chefs dedicate their entire lives to mastering. The least we can do as eaters is understand the basics.

So let me share a few things most visitors get wrong -- and how to fix them.

#### Mistake #1: You're Ignoring Seasonality

In Japan, we have a concept called *shun* -- eating ingredients at their absolute peak of natural flavor. This is not a trend or a marketing gimmick. It is how Japanese food culture has operated for centuries.

When you visit Japan in December and order salmon, you are missing the fact that this is the season of *kan-buri* -- winter yellowtail so rich and sweet it will change your understanding of fish. In autumn, *sanma* (Pacific saury) arrives on menus and the entire country celebrates. In spring, *sakura-dai* (cherry blossom sea bream) is at its delicate, elegant best.

Ask your chef: "Kyou no osusume wa nan desu ka?" -- "What do you recommend today?" That single question unlocks the seasonal magic.

#### Mistake #2: You Only Know Five Fish

Maguro, salmon, hamachi, ebi, unagi. That is the vocabulary of most foreign sushi eaters. And it barely scratches the surface.

There are over 30 common sushi toppings in Japan, and many of them are extraordinary:

**Kohada** (gizzard shad) -- A small, vinegar-cured silver fish that is the ultimate test of a sushi chef's skill. Japanese connoisseurs often judge a restaurant by its kohada before anything else.

**Engawa** (flounder fin muscle) -- Fatty, crunchy, and buttery. A unique textural experience you will not find anywhere else.

**Aji** (horse mackerel) -- Clean, slightly oily, served with ginger instead of wasabi. A gateway to the wonderful world of silver-skinned fish.

**Anago** (saltwater eel) -- Lighter and more delicate than the unagi you know. Simmered, glazed, and so tender it melts into the rice.

**Shirako** (cod milt) -- Yes, it is what you think it is. And yes, it tastes like warm, savory custard from the ocean. A winter luxury that demands you set aside your preconceptions.

These are the items that separate someone who eats sushi from someone who *understands* sushi.

#### Mistake #3: You're Afraid of the Counter

Many visitors stick to conveyor belt sushi because counter sushi feels intimidating. The chef is right there. There is no menu. You do not speak Japanese. What if you do something wrong?

Here is the truth: sushi chefs love customers who are curious and respectful. You do not need to speak Japanese beyond a few key phrases. "Omakase de onegaishimasu" (chef's choice, please) is all it takes to start. The chef will take care of everything from there.

Sit at the counter. Watch the chef work. Eat each piece as it is served. Say "oishii desu" (it's delicious) when you mean it. Say "gochisousama deshita" (thank you for the meal) when you leave. That is all the etiquette you need.

#### Mistake #4: Soy Sauce and Wasabi Crimes

Two rules that will save you from embarrassment:

1. **Dip the fish side, not the rice side.** Turn the nigiri upside down and lightly touch the fish to the soy sauce. This prevents the rice from absorbing too much sauce and falling apart.

2. **Do not mix wasabi into your soy sauce.** The chef has already placed wasabi between the fish and rice. Mixing wasabi into soy sauce destroys the delicate fragrance of real wasabi and creates an over-seasoned mess.

At high-end restaurants, the chef often seasons each piece for you -- no soy sauce needed at all. If you are unsure, watch what the chef does and follow.
