### Q1: What's the best sushi restaurant on a budget in Japan?

URL: https://www.quora.com/Whats-the-best-sushi-restaurant-on-a-budget-in-Japan

回答（コピペ）:

```
I'm Japanese, so I can give you some insider tips.

You don't need to spend a fortune for excellent sushi in Japan. Here are your best options:

**Conveyor belt chains (¥1,000-2,000 per person)**
Sushiro is the best quality among the big chains. Kura Sushi is a close second. Both have plates starting at ¥100-150 each. A full meal of 10 plates costs about ¥1,200. The quality is genuinely good — these chains have massive purchasing power.

**Lunch sets at mid-range restaurants (¥1,500-3,000)**
This is the real hack. Many restaurants that charge ¥10,000+ at dinner offer lunch sets for ¥2,000-3,000. Same fish, same chef, half the price. Look for "ランチ" (lunch) signs outside sushi restaurants between 11am-2pm.

**Standing sushi near train stations (¥1,000-2,000)**
Quick, fresh, and surprisingly good. Pieces are ¥100-300 each. You'll find these in most major stations in Tokyo and Osaka.

**Supermarket sushi after 6pm (¥500-800)**
Japanese supermarket sushi is better than most sushi restaurant meals abroad. After 6pm, many items are discounted 20-50%. A ¥500 pack from a Japanese supermarket is genuinely excellent.

My personal recommendation: find a local sushi restaurant (not a chain, not in a tourist area) and go for the lunch set. Ask "osusume wa nan desu ka?" (what do you recommend?) and let the chef guide you.
```

### Q2: How often do people in Japan eat sushi?

URL: https://www.quora.com/How-often-do-people-in-Japan-eat-sushi

回答（コピペ）:

```
I'm Japanese, so I can speak from actual experience rather than statistics.

Most Japanese people eat sushi about 1-3 times per month. But it depends heavily on the type:

**Conveyor belt sushi (回転寿司)**: This is casual, affordable dining. Families go 2-4 times a month. It's like going to any fast food restaurant — not a special occasion.

**Counter sushi (カウンター寿司)**: This is more like 1-2 times a month for people who enjoy it, or less for most people. Price ranges from ¥3,000 to ¥15,000 depending on the restaurant.

**High-end omakase**: A few times a year at most. This is ¥20,000-50,000+ per person and is reserved for celebrations, business dinners, or special dates.

**Supermarket sushi**: Very frequent. Many people buy supermarket sushi packs weekly as a convenient dinner option.

One thing that surprises visitors: sushi is not the daily staple people imagine. Japanese people eat a huge variety of food — ramen, curry rice, grilled fish, udon, tonkatsu, yakiniku. Sushi is popular but it's one of many options.

What IS daily is rice and fish in general. Just not always in sushi form.
```

回答（コピペ）:

```
I'm Japanese — here are the real prices as of 2026:

First, important note: in Japan, we mostly eat nigiri (fish on rice), not rolls. "Sushi rolls" (maki) are a smaller part of the menu. But here are prices for both:

**Conveyor belt restaurants (Sushiro, Kura Sushi, Hamazushi)**
- Basic plates: ¥120-180 ($0.80-1.20) — 2 pieces per plate
- Premium plates: ¥300-500 ($2-3.30) — otoro, uni, etc.
- A full meal: ¥1,200-2,000 ($8-13)

**Mid-range sushi restaurants**
- Individual nigiri: ¥200-600 ($1.30-4) per piece
- Lunch set (8-10 pieces + soup): ¥1,500-3,000 ($10-20)
- Dinner: ¥5,000-10,000 ($33-66)

**Standing sushi bars**
- Per piece: ¥100-400 ($0.65-2.65)
- Full meal: ¥1,000-2,500 ($6.50-16.50)

**Supermarket sushi packs**
- Basic pack (8-10 pieces): ¥500-800 ($3.30-5.30)
- Premium pack: ¥1,000-1,500 ($6.50-10)
- After 6pm discount: 20-50% off

The biggest price factor is the fish type, not the restaurant type. Tuna (akami) is cheap everywhere. Otoro, uni, and ikura are expensive everywhere.

Pro tip: lunch at a mid-range restaurant gives you the best value-to-quality ratio in Japan.

### Q4: What do Japanese sushi chefs say when you enter?

URL: https://www.quora.com/What-do-Japanese-sushi-chefs-say-when-you-enter

回答（コピペ）:

```
I'm Japanese, so I hear this every time I eat sushi.

When you enter a sushi restaurant, you'll hear the chef (and often the entire staff) shout:

**"Irasshaimase!"** (いらっしゃいませ)

This means "Welcome!" It's not specific to sushi restaurants — you'll hear it in almost every shop and restaurant in Japan. But sushi chefs tend to say it with particular energy and enthusiasm, especially at counter seats.

A few other things you might hear:

**"Hai, irasshai!"** — A more casual, shortened version. Common at standing sushi bars and conveyor belt places.

**"Okaeri nasai"** — "Welcome back." Some regular-focused restaurants say this to returning customers.

**You don't need to respond.** A slight nod or "konnichiwa" is perfectly fine. Most Japanese customers don't verbally respond either.

**When you leave**, the staff will shout:

**"Arigato gozaimashita!"** (ありがとうございました) — "Thank you very much!"

Some phrases that are useful once you're seated:
- "Omakase de onegaishimasu" — Chef's choice, please
- "Osusume wa nan desu ka?" — What do you recommend?
- "Oaiso onegaishimasu" — Check, please
- "Oishii desu" — It's delicious (chefs love hearing this)
```

### Q5: Which is more popular in Japan, sushi or sashimi?

URL: https://www.quora.com/Which-is-more-popular-in-Japan-sushi-or-sashimi

回答（コピペ）:

```
I'm Japanese — this is a great question and the answer might surprise you.

**Sushi is more widely consumed**, but not by as large a margin as you might think.

Here's how they compare:

**Sushi** is more popular as a casual dining option. Conveyor belt sushi chains (Sushiro, Kura Sushi) are everywhere and families eat there regularly. Sushi is also the more common choice for takeout and supermarket purchases.

**Sashimi** is more popular as a home-cooking side dish. Many Japanese households buy sashimi packs at the supermarket and eat them with rice as part of a regular dinner. It's also the standard accompaniment to drinking — if you go to an izakaya (Japanese pub), sashimi is almost always ordered.

The real distinction Japanese people make isn't "sushi vs sashimi" — it's about the occasion:
- Quick family dinner → conveyor belt sushi
- Home dinner with drinks → sashimi pack from supermarket
- Special occasion → omakase sushi at a nice counter
- Business dinner → could be either, often sashimi as a starter followed by other dishes
- With beer/sake → sashimi wins overwhelmingly

Interestingly, many omakase (chef's choice) sushi meals actually begin with sashimi courses before moving to nigiri. So at the highest level, you get both in one meal.

One more thing: the quality difference between sushi and sashimi at the same price point is interesting. Sashimi lets the fish speak entirely for itself — there's no rice or seasoning to hide behind. So truly excellent sashimi requires the highest quality fish.
```

### タイトル:

```
What Most Tourists Get Wrong About Sushi in Japan
```

### 本文:

```
I've watched tourists eat sushi in Tokyo for years, and the same mistakes keep happening. Not rude mistakes — just missed opportunities. Here's what I wish someone had told them.

## You're Eating the Wrong Fish at the Wrong Time

The most common tourist order: salmon, tuna, shrimp. Every time. Regardless of season.

Here's what you're missing: Japanese cuisine revolves around shun (旬) — peak season. Right now in spring, sayori (halfbeak) is at its most delicate and elegant. Shima-aji (striped jack) has a clean, refined flavor that disappears by summer. Hotaru-ika (firefly squid) is available for just a few weeks.

When you order salmon in spring, you're eating something available year-round while ignoring fish that won't be this good again for another twelve months.

The fix is simple. When you sit down, ask: "Osusume wa nan desu ka?" (おすすめは何ですか？) — What do you recommend? The chef will point you to whatever is best TODAY.

## You're Drowning Your Sushi in Soy Sauce

At high-end sushi restaurants, the chef has already seasoned each piece. A thin brush of nikiri (a blend of soy sauce, mirin, and dashi) is applied before the sushi reaches you. Dipping it in soy sauce again is like adding ketchup to a Michelin-starred dish.

At conveyor belt restaurants, soy sauce dipping is fine. But even then, the technique matters: dip the fish side, not the rice side. The rice absorbs too much soy sauce and falls apart.

## The Real Budget Hack

Everyone talks about cheap conveyor belt sushi. That's fine. But the real secret is lunch sets at mid-range restaurants.

Many restaurants that charge ¥15,000 or more at dinner serve lunch for ¥2,500–3,500. Same chef. Same fish from the same market. The only difference is fewer courses and a slightly simpler presentation.

Look for restaurants with "ランチ" (lunch) written on signs outside between 11am and 2pm. Your best sushi experience in Japan might cost less than ¥3,000.

## Three Fish You Need to Try

**Kohada (Gizzard Shad)** — Small, silvery, vinegar-cured. This is the true test of an Edomae sushi chef. If a restaurant serves excellent kohada, everything else will be good too.

**Engawa (Flounder Fin Muscle)** — A thin strip of muscle from the edge of a flounder. Slightly chewy, buttery, with a delicate sweetness. Most tourists don't know it exists.

**Chutoro (Medium Fatty Tuna)** — Everyone orders otoro (the fattiest), but most Japanese regulars prefer chutoro. It has clean tuna flavor with just enough richness. Better balance, and usually half the price.

## Five Phrases That Change Everything

These five phrases will transform your sushi experience:

1. "Omakase de onegaishimasu" (おまかせでお願いします) — Chef's choice, please
2. "Osusume wa nan desu ka?" (おすすめは何ですか？) — What do you recommend?
3. "Oaiso onegaishimasu" (お会計お願いします) — Check, please
4. "Sabi-nuki de" (さび抜きで) — Without wasabi
5. "Oishii desu" (おいしいです) — It's delicious

That last one matters. Sushi chefs spend years mastering their craft. Telling them their sushi is delicious — even in broken Japanese — makes their day.
