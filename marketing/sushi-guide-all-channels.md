```
I've watched thousands of tourists eat sushi in Japan. Most of them make the same mistakes.

They drown their nigiri in soy sauce until the rice falls apart. They mix wasabi into their soy dish like it's soup. They order salmon at every single restaurant because it's the only name they recognize. They skip the extraordinary seasonal fish sitting right in front of them.

I'm Japanese. I was born and raised here, and I've been eating sushi since before I could properly hold chopsticks. Let me share what I wish every visitor knew.

## The Fish You're Missing

Most visitors stick to maguro (tuna), sake (salmon), and maybe ebi (shrimp). That's like visiting France and only eating baguettes.

Here's what you should be trying:

**Kohada (Gizzard Shad)** — This is the true test of an Edomae sushi chef. A small, silvery fish that requires precise vinegar curing. When done right, it's tangy, complex, and utterly addictive. If a restaurant serves excellent kohada, everything else on their menu will be good too.

**Engawa (Flounder Fin Muscle)** — The thin strip of muscle along the edge of a flounder. Slightly chewy, buttery, with a delicate sweetness. Most tourists don't know it exists.

**Chutoro (Medium Fatty Tuna)** — Everyone wants otoro (the fattiest cut), but most Japanese regulars I know prefer chutoro. It has the clean tuna flavor with just enough richness. The fat is present but doesn't overwhelm.

**Shima-aji (Striped Jack)** — Available mainly in spring and summer. Clean, elegant, with a firm texture. One of the most refined white fish in the sushi repertoire.

## The Seasonal Secret

Japanese food culture revolves around "shun" — peak season. The same fish tastes completely different depending on when you eat it.

- **Spring**: Sayori (halfbeak), shima-aji, hotaru-ika (firefly squid)
- **Summer**: Aji (horse mackerel), uni (sea urchin from Hokkaido), anago (conger eel)
- **Autumn**: Sanma (pacific saury), ikura (salmon roe), saba (mackerel)
- **Winter**: Buri (yellowtail), hirame (flounder), fugu (pufferfish)

When you sit at a sushi counter, the single best question you can ask is: "Osusume wa nan desu ka?" (What do you recommend?). The chef will point you to what's at peak flavor today.

## The Budget Hack Nobody Talks About

You don't need to spend ¥30,000 for great sushi. Here's the secret: many mid-range restaurants that charge ¥15,000+ at dinner offer the same quality for ¥3,000-5,000 at lunch.

And conveyor belt sushi in Japan is genuinely good. Sushiro, Kura Sushi, and Hamazushi have massive purchasing power. A full meal of 8-12 plates costs ¥1,000-2,000. Go at off-peak hours (2-4pm) when everything is freshly made.
```

### Q2: "How often do people in Japan eat sushi?"
URL: https://www.quora.com/How-often-do-people-in-Japan-eat-sushi
```
I'm Japanese. Honestly, it depends on the person, but most Japanese people eat sushi 1-4 times per month.

Conveyor belt sushi (kaiten-zushi) is a casual family meal — we go on weekends the way Americans go to a diner. It's affordable (¥1,000-2,000 per person) and fun.

High-end counter sushi (omakase) is more of a special occasion — birthdays, business dinners, celebrations. Maybe 2-4 times per year.

What might surprise you: many Japanese people eat sushi at home. Supermarket sushi packs are genuinely good here, and "temaki party" (hand-roll party where everyone makes their own) is a popular family dinner.
```

### Q3: "What is the average cost of a sushi roll in Japan?"
URL: https://www.quora.com/What-is-the-average-cost-of-a-sushi-roll-in-Japan-not-including-high-end-restaurants
```
Japanese local here. Quick breakdown:

- Conveyor belt (kaiten): ¥100-350 per plate (2 pieces). Full meal: ¥1,000-2,000
- Standing sushi bar: ¥100-500 per piece
- Mid-range counter: ¥300-800 per piece, or lunch set ¥2,000-5,000
- High-end omakase: ¥15,000-50,000+ for full course

Important note: in Japan, "sushi rolls" (maki) are much simpler than American-style rolls. A hosomaki (thin roll with one filling like tuna or cucumber) costs ¥100-200. The elaborate inside-out rolls with cream cheese don't really exist here.

Most visitors get the best value from lunch sets at mid-range restaurants.
```

### Q4: "What do Japanese sushi chefs say when you enter?"
URL: https://www.quora.com/What-do-Japanese-sushi-chefs-say-when-you-enter
```
Japanese here. They shout "Irasshaimase!" (いらっしゃいませ) — it means "Welcome!"

You don't need to reply to this. Just nod or smile. It's a greeting, not a question.

Here are a few more essential phrases for the sushi counter:

- "Omakase de onegaishimasu" = Chef's choice, please
- "Osusume wa nan desu ka?" = What do you recommend?
- "Oaiso onegaishimasu" = Check, please
- "Sabi-nuki de" = Without wasabi
- "Oishii desu" = It's delicious (chefs love hearing this)
```

### Q5: "Which is more popular in Japan, sushi or sashimi?"
URL: https://www.quora.com/Which-is-more-popular-in-Japan-sushi-or-sashimi
```
I'm Japanese. Sushi is more popular as a standalone meal. Sashimi is more popular as an accompaniment to drinks.

When we go out specifically to eat, we go for sushi. When we're at an izakaya drinking beer or sake, we order a sashimi platter as one of many small dishes.

At home, sashimi is actually more common — you can buy a pack at the supermarket and eat it with rice as a quick dinner. Sushi requires more preparation (the rice needs to be seasoned).

One thing that surprises visitors: in Japan, it's completely normal to order both sashimi AND sushi at a sushi counter. Start with sashimi and sake, then move to nigiri. That's the traditional way.
```
